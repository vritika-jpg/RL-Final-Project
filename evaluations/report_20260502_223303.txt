OVERVIEW
This report evaluates Q-learning and SARSA trained on a 27-state inventory replenishment problem (3 inventory x 3 demand x 3 cash levels, 4 order actions) over 5,000 episodes. Both algorithms use identical hyperparameters (alpha=0.1, gamma=0.95, epsilon 1.0->0.05) and are evaluated against four baselines across 500 test episodes from a neutral start state.

LEARNING CURVES
Q-learning reached 80% of its final smoothed reward by episode 0, while SARSA reached the same threshold at episode 2. Q-learning converged faster, consistent with off-policy learning allowing it to target the greedy policy directly during training. By the final 500 training episodes, Q-learning averaged 3026 reward per episode versus SARSA at 2323, a late-stage gap of 703 points.

Both agents correctly order less aggressively under tight cash. SARSA orders more aggressively under ample cash (avg action 1.89 vs 0.89), consistent with its on-policy optimism.

RECOMMENDATION
Recommended model: Q-learning.
Q-learning achieves the highest average reward among RL agents (3026), a 703-point lead over SARSA, with a stockout rate of 65.4% and holding cost of 1081. It converged to a stable policy by episode 0 and generalises sensibly across all cash states. While Order-up-to (3035) is competitive, it is a hand-crafted heuristic that ignores cash availability. Q-learning learns this behaviour from experience and is better suited for environments where cash constraints actively shape replenishment decisions.

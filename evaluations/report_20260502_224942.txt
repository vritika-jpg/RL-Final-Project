OVERVIEW
This report evaluates Q-learning and SARSA trained on a 27-state inventory replenishment problem (3 inventory x 3 demand x 3 cash levels, 4 order actions) over 5,000 episodes. Both algorithms use identical hyperparameters (alpha=0.1, gamma=0.95, epsilon 1.0->0.05) and are evaluated against four baselines across 500 test episodes from a neutral start state.

LEARNING CURVES
Q-learning reached 80% of its learning gap at episode 330, while SARSA reached the same threshold at episode 581. Q-learning converged faster, consistent with off-policy learning allowing it to target the greedy policy directly during training. By the final 500 training episodes, Q-learning averaged 2869 reward per episode versus SARSA at 2230, a late-stage gap of 639 points.

RECOMMENDATION
Recommended model: Q-learning.
Q-learning achieves the highest average reward among RL agents (2869), a 639-point lead over SARSA, with a stockout rate of 66.8% and holding cost of 1110. It converged to a stable policy by episode 330 and generalises sensibly across all cash states. While Order-up-to (3056) is competitive, it is a hand-crafted heuristic that ignores cash availability. Q-learning learns this behaviour from experience and is better suited for environments where cash constraints actively shape replenishment decisions.
